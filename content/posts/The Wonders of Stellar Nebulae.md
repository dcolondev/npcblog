A stellar nebula, also known as a star-forming region, is an interstellar cloud of dust, hydrogen, helium, and other ionized gases. ## What is a Stellar Nebula?

At the heart of a stellar nebula lies a dense molecular cloud, suspended in the vastness of space. This cloud, composed mostly of hydrogen, serves as the birthplace and breeding ground for new stars. Within these cosmic nurseries, gravity begins to pack the gas and dust together, creating immense pressure and temperature.

### Types of Stellar Nebulae

There are several types of stellar nebulae, each with its own unique characteristics. Here are a few notable ones:

1. **Emission Nebulae**: These nebulae are predominantly composed of ionized gases that emit light of various wavelengths, resulting in vibrant, colorful displays.

2. **Reflection Nebulae**: Unlike emission nebulae, reflection nebulae simply reflect the light of nearby stars, creating a bluish glow.

3. **Dark Nebulae**: These dense clouds of non-luminous matter obscure the light from stars behind them, resulting in dark patches on the sky.

## Star Formation and Planetary Systems

Within stellar nebulae, the densest areas eventually collapse under their own gravitational force. As the cloud collapses, it fragments into numerous clumps, each destined to become a star. These infant stars are surrounded by protoplanetary disks, swirling discs of gas and dust. Over time, these disks coalesce into planets, asteroids, and other celestial bodies, forming intricate planetary systems.

### Interesting Nebulae Facts

- The famous Orion Nebula is one of the most studied stellar nurseries, located in the constellation of Orion.

- Stellar nebulae provide us with valuable insights into the origins of our own Solar System and the formation of Earth.

- The light from stellar nebulae takes thousands to millions of years to reach Earth, allowing us to glimpse into the ancient past.
